# 1.2.2 Emotional & Experiential Goals

Infinite Worlds should make players feel that they are entering a place with its own memory, pressure, beauty, and moral weight. The target emotion is not constant empowerment. It is a cycle of curiosity, vulnerability, competence, responsibility, and awe.

The experience should begin with grounded presence: the player notices wind, texture, distance, sound direction, hand position, tool weight, weather change, and NPC attention before they think about abstract stats. Discovery should feel earned through observation. A strange plant, unusual animal behavior, lunar alignment, or rumor from an NPC should invite investigation without immediately becoming a checklist marker.

The world should also create tension without flattening into punishment. Survival risk, permanent consequences, social memory, and dangerous magic should make decisions feel meaningful, but players need enough readable feedback to understand what happened and how they might prepare differently next time. Fear works best when it comes from uncertainty and consequence, not opaque failure.

Core emotional targets:

- Wonder: landscapes, ruins, weather, celestial events, and impossible spaces should suggest that the world is larger than the player.
- Belonging: camps, settlements, mentors, companions, and repeated places should give players emotional anchors.
- Responsibility: choices should visibly affect NPC trust, ecological state, faction response, and future opportunities.
- Grief and legacy: death, loss, abandoned places, and remembered actions should change the world without ending its continuity.
- Mastery through practice: players should feel themselves becoming more capable because they learned the world, not because they only unlocked a number.

Implementation mapping: the first testable version should appear through small, inspectable moments in the player experience contract, wind test island, interaction loop, NPC memory prototypes, and living-world harnesses. Each emotional goal needs at least one observable cue, one player action, and one validation or playtest note before it becomes a production requirement.

Open questions:

- Which emotional beats must appear in the first vertical slice?
- How much hardship can the first prototype include before it distracts from testing core interaction?
- What telemetry or QA-live evidence can confirm that players noticed systemic feedback instead of treating it as decoration?
