# 1.2.4 Making Games that Stand Out

Here’s a two-part dive: first a full summary of Nick Popovich’s “Making Games That Stand Out and Survive” GDC talk, then a synthesis of best practices—drawn from VR-focused guides and indie-dev wisdom—framed around how you can apply them in your own VR project.

––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––

Part 1: “Making Games That Stand Out and Survive” (Nick Popovich, GDC 2019)[^1^]

1. Attention Is Your Currency
    
    • Players don’t buy games—they trade you their most precious resource: *time*.
    
    • With thousands of titles launched every month, your first job is to *grab* a few seconds of attention[^1^].
    
2. Sell It with a GIF
    
    • A single looping GIF must convey three things:
    
    1. The **core gameplay loop** (“what you do every 30 seconds”).
    2. The **killer app** (“what makes it unique”).
    3. The **emotional hook** (“how it feels”).
    • Examples: Spelunky’s cave-diving peril; Superhot’s time-bending freeze; Rocket League’s car-soccer mashup[^1^].
3. Stay Relevant with Ongoing Engagement
    
    • **Early Access & Feedback**: let passionate fans into your build, listen, iterate.
    
    • **Regular Content Drops**: seasons, events, live ops—give players reasons to return.
    
    • **Community Fuel**: fan art features, social-media spotlights and Twitch integrations keep your audience invested[^1^].
    
4. Give Players a “Place to Call Home”
    
    • Design a low-stress hub (base, ranch, village) where players can unwind, customize and feel ownership.
    
    • Balance core loop stress (combat, puzzles) with restful loops (decorating, farming, socializing) to broaden appeal[^1^].
    
5. Find Your Voice & Vision
    
    • It’s not enough to imitate—your game must *say* something with its mechanics, art and tone.
    
    • Cultivate a distinctive aesthetic or narrative twist that resonates emotionally and makes you *memorable*[^1^].

Part 2: Lessons from VR-Dev & Indie Guides—Turning Popovich into VR Action

A. Start with a Compelling, VR-Native Hook

• Define one “why VR?” feature: physics-based object interactions, room-scale exploration, haptic puzzles[^42^].

• Prototype that hook early. If teleportation + puzzle-solving is your killer app, build a short loop to GIF and share it immediately on social media[^1^][^42^].

B. Emphasize Presence & Comfort

• Guard 90+ FPS and sub-20 ms latency—nothing kills attention like motion sickness[^40^].

• Offer multiple locomotion options (smooth locomotion, blink teleport, arm-swing)—let players pick their comfort zone[^41^].

C. Craft a VR “Home Base”

• Mirror Popovich’s “place to call home” by giving players a customizable VR space—a workshop, lab or cozy outpost.

• Use serialized unlocks (new décor items, tool modules) as low-pressure rewards between intense sessions[^1^][^41^].

D. Build & Nurture Your Community

• Release **early prototypes** to small VR communities (Reddit r/VRDev, Discord servers) for feedback[^40^].

• Showcase player-made VR gifs and 360° clips—treat them as your primary marketing content[^1^].

E. Leverage Modular, Iterative Design

• Scope your first demo tight: pick one environment, one puzzle or one combat encounter. Polish it fully, then *add* content in themed modules—“Episode 1: The Abandoned Station,” “Episode 2: The Forgotten Workshop,” etc.[^41^].

• Each module becomes a GIF-worthy moment and a reason for players to come back[^1^].

F. Design for Shareability

• VR excels at spectacle—identify those “wow” moments (giant robot fights, gravity-bending puzzles) and build camera-friendly angles.

• Embed a “share” gesture or UI button so players can post short VR clips directly to socials[^1^].

G. Align Your Tone & Aesthetic

• VR’s intimacy magnifies atmosphere—choose a cohesive art style and sound design that underscores your theme (no jarring asset mashups)[^3^].

• Commission a small batch of bespoke assets (unique shaders, custom hand models) rather than relying solely on stock packs[^3^].

H. Iterate with Real-Time Metrics

• Instrument playtests to record gaze heatmaps, interaction frequency and motion triggers. Use this data to refine your core loop and GIF moments[^1^].

• Tie analytics back to content decisions: if 70 % of players drop in the first minute of your hub, simplify its onboarding[^4^].

I. Keep Your Vision & Roadmap Visible

• Publish a public dev roadmap (Treasure map format) so backers know when new modules, mechanics or “home base” upgrades drop.

• Consistent updates—even small ones—signal you’re committed and keep your player base engaged[^1^].

––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––

By combining Popovich’s high-level marketing and engagement ethos with VR-specific comfort, immersion and iteration practices, you’ll not only *stand out* in a crowded marketplace but also *survive* and grow your VR community over the long haul.
